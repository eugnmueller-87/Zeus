# Sector Dynamics — How Each Sector Responds to Events

## Technology (XLK)
Characteristics: high beta, sensitive to rates and risk appetite, driven by earnings and innovation cycles.

Supply chain disruptions: CRITICAL impact. Tech is deeply dependent on semiconductor supply (TSMC, ASML, NVIDIA). A chip shortage cascades through the entire sector.
Earnings surprises: very high price movement. Tech stocks move 10-20% on earnings. High opportunity but also high risk.
Regulatory action: antitrust and data privacy are the primary risks. EU is more aggressive than US on tech regulation (GDPR, Digital Markets Act).
Interest rate sensitivity: high. When rates rise, high-P/E tech stocks de-rate sharply. Always check macro context before going long tech in a rising-rate environment.

Key tickers monitored by Hermes: NVIDIA, Apple, Microsoft, Intel, Qualcomm, TSMC, SAP.

## Energy (XLE)
Characteristics: driven by commodity prices (oil, gas), geopolitics, OPEC decisions.

Supply chain disruptions: bullish for energy producers (less supply = higher prices). Short refiners and airlines who are buyers of energy.
Geopolitical events: highest impact sector. Any Middle East tension, Russia sanctions, or pipeline disruption moves energy stocks 5-15%.
ESG pressure: long-term headwind. Energy companies face regulatory and capital allocation pressure. Do not hold for weeks-long periods.
Seasonality: energy demand peaks in Q4 (winter heating) and Q2 (driving season). Align signals with seasonal bias.

## Financials (XLF)
Characteristics: sensitive to interest rates, credit conditions, regulatory environment.

Rate sensitivity: banks earn more in high-rate environments (wider net interest margin). Rising rates = bullish for banks.
Regulatory action: BAFIN (Germany), ECB, SEC actions move financial stocks significantly. Watch for capital requirement changes.
Earnings surprises: loan loss provisions are the key variable. Higher provisions = miss. Lower = beat.
Systemic risk signals: any signal suggesting banking sector stress (SVB-type events) should trigger immediate risk reduction across all positions.

## Healthcare (XLV)
Characteristics: defensive, low correlation with market, driven by FDA decisions and clinical trial results.

Regulatory action (FDA): FDA approval → +30-100% on small biotech. FDA rejection → -50-80%. Binary events. ZEUS should not hold positions through binary FDA decisions.
Earnings: relatively predictable for large pharma. Surprising for biotech.
Macro correlation: low. Healthcare is a defensive sector. In bear markets, rotate to healthcare.
EU-specific: EMA (European Medicines Agency) decisions matter for EU-listed healthcare stocks.

## Industrials (XLI)
Characteristics: correlated with economic cycle, sensitive to trade policy and supply chains.

Supply chain disruptions: direct impact. Industrials are deep in the supply chain — disruptions propagate quickly.
Trade policy: tariffs and trade wars hit industrials hard (cross-border supply chains, export markets).
German industrials: Siemens, BASF, Volkswagen, BMW are heavily monitored by Hermes. Germany is the world's export engine — trade signals have outsized impact here.

## Consumer Discretionary
Characteristics: highly correlated with consumer confidence and disposable income.

Earnings: most important signal. Consumer discretionary is very earnings-driven.
Supply chain: important for retail (inventory issues = margin compression or stock-outs).
Macro sensitivity: bear markets hurt consumer discretionary most. Defensive sectors outperform.
